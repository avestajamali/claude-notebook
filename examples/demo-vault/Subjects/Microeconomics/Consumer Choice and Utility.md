## Why This Lecture Matters

Why do you buy two coffees but not five? Utility theory explains how people spread a limited budget across goods to get the most satisfaction. It is the engine underneath the demand curve from [[Supply and Demand]] — once you see *why* demand slopes down, the whole model clicks into place.

---

## 1. Total vs Marginal Utility

- **Total utility** is the overall satisfaction from consuming a good.
- **Marginal utility** ($MU$) is the *extra* satisfaction from one more unit.

The key fact is **diminishing marginal utility**: each extra unit adds less than the one before. Your first slice of pizza is bliss; the fifth is a chore.

## 2. The Equimarginal Rule

To spend a budget optimally, a consumer balances the *bang per buck* across goods:

$$\frac{MU_x}{P_x} = \frac{MU_y}{P_y}$$

If one good gives more utility per dollar, shift spending toward it until the ratios equalise.

## 3. Worked Example

You have goods X and Y. Currently:

$$\frac{MU_x}{P_x} = \frac{20}{2} = 10 \qquad \frac{MU_y}{P_y} = \frac{18}{3} = 6$$

X delivers 10 utils per dollar versus Y's 6, so **buy more X**. As you consume more X, $MU_x$ falls (diminishing utility) until the two ratios meet — that is your optimal mix.

> [!warning]
> The rule compares utility *per dollar*, not raw utility. A good with huge $MU$ can still be a bad buy if its price is high enough.

## 4. Link to Demand

When the price of X rises, $\frac{MU_x}{P_x}$ falls, so the consumer buys less X. That is exactly the downward-sloping demand curve behind [[Elasticity of Demand]] — utility theory is its foundation.

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] Marginal utility diminishes as you consume more.
- [ ] Optimal choice equalises $MU/P$ across all goods.
- [ ] Diminishing marginal utility is *why* demand slopes down.
