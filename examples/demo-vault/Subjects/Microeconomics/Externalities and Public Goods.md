## Why This Lecture Matters

Sometimes markets get the price right for buyers and sellers but wrong for *everyone else*. A factory that pollutes a river, a neighbour who plants a beautiful garden — these spill costs and benefits onto third parties. This is where the tidy equilibrium of [[Supply and Demand]] breaks down and government has a genuine role.

---

## 1. Externalities

An **externality** is a cost or benefit that falls on someone not part of the transaction.

- **Negative** (pollution, congestion): the market produces *too much*.
- **Positive** (vaccination, education): the market produces *too little*.

The private price ignores the external cost, so the market quantity is wrong for society.

## 2. Correcting the Gap

A **Pigouvian tax** raises the private cost to match the social cost, nudging output back to the efficient level. The ideal tax equals the external harm per unit:

$$\text{Tax} = MSC - MPC$$

where $MSC$ is marginal *social* cost and $MPC$ is marginal *private* cost.

## 3. Worked Example

A factory's private cost of a unit is \$40. Each unit also dumps \$15 of pollution on the town, so the social cost is \$55.

$$\text{Optimal tax} = MSC - MPC = 55 - 40 = \boxed{\$15}$$

A \$15-per-unit tax makes the firm *feel* the pollution it causes, cutting output to the socially efficient level.

> [!warning]
> Externalities are a market *failure*, not a market flaw in pricing. The buyer and seller are behaving rationally — the problem is that a third party's costs never entered the price.

## 4. Public Goods

A **public good** is non-excludable and non-rival — national defence, street lighting. Because no one can be charged, private firms won't supply it (the free-rider problem), so government usually steps in. This is a different failure from the pricing power seen in [[Market Structures]], but both explain why real markets need oversight.

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] Negative externalities → overproduction; positive → underproduction.
- [ ] A Pigouvian tax equal to the external harm restores efficiency.
- [ ] Public goods are non-excludable and non-rival, so markets underprovide them.
