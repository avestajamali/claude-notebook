## Why This Tutorial Matters

The lecture gives you the formula; the tutorial is where you *use* it. Elasticity questions almost always ask you to compute a number and then interpret what it means for revenue — this is exactly the two-step exam pattern.

---

## Q1. Compute the Elasticity

A streaming service raises its price from \$10 to \$12 (a 20% rise). Subscribers fall from 500,000 to 450,000 (a 10% fall).

$$E_d = \frac{-10\%}{+20\%} = \boxed{0.5}$$

Since $E_d < 1$, demand is **inelastic** — subscribers barely react. See [[Elasticity of Demand]] for the elastic/inelastic cutoff.

## Q2. Predict the Revenue Effect

- Old revenue: $10 \times 500{,}000 = \$5.0$m
- New revenue: $12 \times 450{,}000 = \$5.4$m

Revenue *rose*. With inelastic demand, a price increase raises revenue — the volume lost is more than offset by the higher price.

> [!tip]
> Whenever you get an elasticity below 1, expect a price rise to *increase* revenue. This links straight back to pricing power in [[Market Structures]].

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] $E_d < 1$ → raising price raises revenue.
- [ ] Always pair the number with its revenue interpretation.
