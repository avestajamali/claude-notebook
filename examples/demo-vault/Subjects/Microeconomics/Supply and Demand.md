## Why This Lecture Matters

Almost every price you see — coffee, rent, airfares — is set where supply meets demand. If you understand this one diagram, you can predict which way a price moves when something changes: a bad harvest, a new tax, a viral trend. It is the single most-used model in economics, and every later topic builds on it.

---

## 1. The Two Curves

- **Demand** slopes *down*: as price falls, buyers want more.
- **Supply** slopes *up*: as price rises, sellers offer more.

Where they cross is the **equilibrium** — the one price at which the quantity buyers want equals the quantity sellers offer. There is no shortage and no surplus, so the price has no reason to move.

## 2. Shifts vs Movements

A change in *price* moves you **along** a curve. A change in anything else (income, tastes, input costs) **shifts** the whole curve.

> [!tip]
> Rule of thumb: price changes = movement along; everything else = a shift. Mixing these up is the most common exam mistake.

## 3. Worked Example

Suppose the market for wall posters is described by:

$$Q_d = 100 - 2P \qquad Q_s = 20 + 2P$$

At equilibrium $Q_d = Q_s$:

$$100 - 2P = 20 + 2P \;\Rightarrow\; 80 = 4P \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{P = 20}$$

Plug back in: $Q = 100 - 2(20) = 60$ posters. So the market clears at **\$20 and 60 posters**.

## 4. Why Responsiveness Matters

*How much* quantity shifts when price changes is governed by [[Elasticity of Demand]]. A steep (inelastic) demand curve means a supply shock moves price a lot; a flat (elastic) one means it moves quantity a lot. The same equilibrium logic later explains pricing under [[Market Structures]].

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] Equilibrium is where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
- [ ] Price changes cause *movements*; other factors cause *shifts*.
- [ ] Solve for equilibrium by setting $Q_d = Q_s$.
