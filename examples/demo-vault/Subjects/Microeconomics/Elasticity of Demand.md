## Why This Lecture Matters

Price elasticity of demand measures how much quantity demanded responds to a price change. If a 10% price rise cuts quantity by 25%, elasticity is 2.5 — demand is elastic, and the firm's revenue *falls* when it raises prices. Getting this number right is the difference between a price increase that adds profit and one that destroys it.

---

## 1. Definition

$$E_d = \frac{\%\,\Delta Q_d}{\%\,\Delta P}$$

- **Elastic** ($E_d > 1$): quantity responds strongly. Luxuries, goods with close substitutes.
- **Inelastic** ($E_d < 1$): quantity barely moves. Necessities, few substitutes.
- **Unit elastic** ($E_d = 1$): revenue is unchanged by a price move.

## 2. Worked Example

A cafe raises coffee from $5.00 to $5.50 (a 10% rise). Daily sales fall from 400 to 360 cups (a 10% fall).

$$E_d = \frac{-10\%}{+10\%} = \boxed{1.0}$$

Unit elastic — revenue is unchanged ($2,000/day either way). The cafe gains nothing from the price rise on volume alone.

> [!tip]
> Total revenue is maximised where $E_d = 1$. Above it, cutting price raises revenue; below it, raising price does.

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] Elasticity compares *percentage* changes, not absolute ones.
- [ ] Elastic demand → price and revenue move in opposite directions.
