## Why This Lecture Matters

The number of firms in a market changes everything about price. A wheat farmer is a price *taker* — one of thousands, powerless to set the price. A patent-holding drug maker is a price *maker*. Knowing where a business sits on this spectrum tells you whether it can raise prices without losing every customer.

---

## 1. Perfect Competition

Many small firms, identical products, free entry and exit. Each firm faces a flat demand curve and must accept the market price. Profit is maximised where:

$$P = MC$$

Because entry is free, long-run economic profit is competed away to zero.

## 2. Monopoly

A single seller with a unique product and barriers to entry. The monopolist faces the *whole* market demand curve, so to sell more it must cut price. It maximises profit where:

$$MR = MC \quad\text{with}\quad P > MC$$

The gap between $P$ and $MC$ is market power — the ability to price above cost.

## 3. Worked Example

A monopolist faces demand $P = 100 - Q$ and constant marginal cost $MC = 20$.

Total revenue is $TR = P \times Q = (100 - Q)Q$, so marginal revenue is $MR = 100 - 2Q$. Set $MR = MC$:

$$100 - 2Q = 20 \;\Rightarrow\; Q = 40, \quad P = 100 - 40 = \boxed{60}$$

The monopolist charges **\$60** — far above the \$20 marginal cost. Under perfect competition, price would have been driven down to \$20.

> [!tip]
> Same demand curve, very different price. Whether a firm can hold price above cost depends on how [[Elasticity of Demand]] shapes the demand it faces — elastic demand punishes markups.

## 4. The Spectrum

Real markets sit between these poles: monopolistic competition (cafes, salons) and oligopoly (airlines, telcos). The equilibrium logic from [[Supply and Demand]] still applies — market structure just changes how much power each firm has over price.

## Key Takeaways
- [ ] Perfect competition: price takers, $P = MC$, zero long-run profit.
- [ ] Monopoly: price maker, $MR = MC$, $P > MC$.
- [ ] Market power is the ability to price above marginal cost.
