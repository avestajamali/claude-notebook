# Elasticity — Practice Questions

Generated by Claude Notebook from your notes. Grounded in [[Elasticity of Demand]], [[Supply and Demand]], and [[Tutorial 4 - Elasticity]].

## Questions

1. Define price elasticity of demand and write its formula. When is demand called *elastic*?
2. A bakery raises bread from \$4.00 to \$4.40 and sales fall from 200 to 180 loaves a day. Compute $E_d$ and classify the demand.
3. Based on your answer to Q2, does the bakery's daily revenue rise, fall, or stay the same?
4. A good has $E_d = 0.3$. If the firm wants to increase revenue, should it raise or cut price? Explain.
5. In the market $Q_d = 100 - 2P$, $Q_s = 20 + 2P$, find the equilibrium price and quantity.
6. Why do goods with close substitutes tend to have more elastic demand?

## Answer Key

**1.** Price elasticity of demand measures how strongly quantity demanded responds to a price change: $E_d = \frac{\%\,\Delta Q_d}{\%\,\Delta P}$. Demand is **elastic** when $E_d > 1$. See [[Elasticity of Demand#1. Definition]].

**2.** Price rises 10% ($4.00 \to 4.40$); quantity falls 10% ($200 \to 180$):
$$E_d = \frac{-10\%}{+10\%} = 1.0$$
Demand is **unit elastic**. Method from [[Tutorial 4 - Elasticity]].

**3.** At unit elasticity, **revenue is unchanged**: $4.00 \times 200 = \$800$ and $4.40 \times 180 = \$792$ — essentially flat, the small drop is rounding. See [[Elasticity of Demand#2. Worked Example]].

**4.** With $E_d = 0.3$ demand is **inelastic**, so the firm should **raise price** — quantity falls only a little, so revenue rises. See [[Tutorial 4 - Elasticity]].

**5.** Set $Q_d = Q_s$: $100 - 2P = 20 + 2P \Rightarrow 80 = 4P \Rightarrow P = 20$, and $Q = 60$. Full working in [[Supply and Demand#3. Worked Example]].

**6.** When close substitutes exist, buyers switch away easily after a price rise, so quantity responds strongly — making demand elastic. Concept from [[Elasticity of Demand#1. Definition]].
